Daniel Bennett

I am a Lecturer (Assistant professor) in Human-Computer Interaction (HCI), at the University of Bristol.

My research connects the three strands of my academic background - philosophy, computer science, and cognitive science. I investigate how technologies impact human autonomy.

Philosophy and psychology tell us that the experience of agency and autonomy is key to individual well-being, enjoyment and success in activities, and even the proper functioning of society. However, these concepts are complex and multifaceted. They include the sense of steering your own life, acting on your own initiative, and having influence, but also the feeling of being powerful and effective in a game, and the experience of choice and self-expression in a social media app.

How do these things relate to each other? Which aspects of agency and autonomy really matter? Are they ever in conflict? Can technologies even exploit our feelings of control and efficacy to drive choices and kinds of engagement we might (paradoxically) regret?

I apply perspectives from philosophy and psychology to disentangle the various aspects of user autonomy and understand how technologies can support users in the ways that matter. I investigate how we can use data to help users develop behaviours and competencies they personally value, understand and pursue their own goals, and navigate the “ultra-processed environments” of the attention economy.
